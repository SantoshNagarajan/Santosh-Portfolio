the problem

Prepaid customers top up when their phone is already dead.

Every step in the old flow was a reason to abandon. At 10 million users, even a 1% improvement in completion rate is a significant number.

01
Login required to pay
Customers in an urgent moment hit a password wall. Most abandoned immediately or called support instead.
Highest drop-off
02
Refill cards and debit only
No Apple Pay, no PayPal. Users had to find a physical card mid-urgency. Most didn't have one ready.
Major friction
03
No split payments
$45 on one card, $20 refill card — impossible to combine. Prepaid customers on tight budgets had no path forward.
Unmet need
04
Called support instead
The most common fallback. Support calls cost Verizon significantly more per transaction than self-serve digital.
Costly workaround
the shift

I went in thinking this was an authentication problem. It wasn't.

Early research focused on making login faster — biometrics, remembered sessions. Then the interviews stopped me. Users didn't want a better login. They wanted no login at all.

Old assumption

Make the login faster and users will complete payment

Biometrics, remembered sessions, streamlined authentication — fix the friction inside the login wall.

The reframe

Remove the login wall entirely — security moves to the backend

A prepaid top-up doesn't need account authentication. Phone number + payment method = sufficient. Backend fraud detection handles the rest, invisibly.

"When your service is down, you don't want to remember a password. You just want to pay."

Payment speed is a trust signal.

A slow checkout made users feel the transaction wasn't going through — even when it was. Animations and real-time feedback weren't polish, they were functional.

Split payments weren't an edge case.

For a significant segment of prepaid users, $45 on one card and a $20 refill card was normal. The flow had to support real budgeting behaviour.

Wallet options were expected, not impressive.

Users were surprised Apple Pay and PayPal weren't already there. Their absence felt like a bug, not a missing feature.

85% of top-ups happened on mobile.

Usually one-handed, while doing something else. Every interaction had to work at arm's length on a small screen.

solution

Five journeys. One principle: get out of the way.

Refill card only, split payment, PayPal, Apple Pay, and credit/debit card. Each journey had its own requirements — but every decision came back to the same question: what does the user already know, and how much can we skip?

1

No sign-in flow

Phone number + one-time code replaces login entirely. Visual trust cues do the work of authentication without a password wall.

2

Expanded payment methods

Apple Pay, PayPal, and Google Pay alongside cards. Tab switching, not a dropdown. Icons and one-line descriptions for instant scanning on mobile.

3

Split payment accordion

Progressive disclosure — the secondary payment option only appears when refill cards don't cover the full amount. Users who only need refill cards never see the complexity.

4

Microinteractions that build trust

Animated loading, real-time inline error handling, and a success screen with receipt sharing. In a payment context, a smooth animation after "Continue" is the difference between confidence and anxiety.

the screens

The split payment screen was the hardest to get right.

A user adds refill cards totalling $130 of the $250 maximum. The accordion opens a secondary payment selector. A live counter tells them exactly where they stand. The total never surprises them at checkout.

reflection

The design took three days. The alignment took six weeks.

That ratio is the reality of shipping in a regulated financial product. Every decision had to survive review by Fraud, Legal, Accessibility, and Brand.

The slider was more fun. The input field was more clear.

Early split payment prototypes used a drag slider. Felt delightful. Usability testing killed it — users wanted to type a specific number, not estimate by dragging. I fell in love with the interaction before validating the need.

Fraud/Risk aren't blockers. They're co-designers.

Every constraint they introduced had a legitimate reason. Learning to design within those constraints — rather than around them — made the final product better, not worse.

The hardest part wasn't the UI.

It was making backend security invisible to the user. Trust is a design decision before it's a security implementation. Every visual, every word, every animation is either building or eroding it.
